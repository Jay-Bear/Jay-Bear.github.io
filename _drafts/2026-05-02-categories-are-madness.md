# Category Theory Is the Language of Madness
*or:* **A Loveletter to Some Symbols and Arrows**

---

A great ape notices that the bright thing in the sky disappears every night.
They panic. Another great ape counts how long until it returns and boom:
civilization.

Mathematics is what happens when pattern recognition develops a drinking
problem. It began very simply, two beers plus two beers is four beers for the
lads. Very comforting. The universe – as it appeared – could be made to sit
down, shut up, and answer questions. Then these great apes got ambitious.

They invented zero, which is nothing but useful. Negative numbers, which is
less than nothing but more than useful. Irrational numbers, which refuse to be
nice nice fractions out of sheer petty spite. Imaginary numbers which don't
really exist until they start running your country's electric grid.

At every stage, mathematics did something deeply unsettling: it worked. Not
just *kinda worked*, not just *"eehhh, if we ignore some things it's mostly
fine"*... no, it describes how the planets move, when bridges stay up, where
you're likely to lose a bet, and why advertising companies know more about you
than your family does.

You begin counting sheep, and – *provided you haven't fallen asleep* – do a
little bit more to figure out the temperature of a star that exploded before
sheep existed. It quite frankly feels like cheating.

Yet mathematics isn't so much discovered as it is cornered.

Most people think mathematics is about numbers. This is understandable. It's
also like thinking literature is about vowels.

School often presents mathematics as a bureaucratic obstacle course. *"Jim is
on a 110 mph train from London Euston to Manchester Piccadilly. Anji is
strapped to an ICBM to be launched from Nova Scotia to the same destination as
Jim. If they both depart at the same time, how tall is Imhotep?"*

You memorize formulas. You perform rituals. You carry the one, despite it never
telling you where it wants to go. If you're very lucky, someone tells you
being *'bad at math'* is a personality trait like enjoying jazz or collecting
figurines.

But mathematics is about arithmetic as music is about scales. Arithmetic is a
gateway drug to the study of structure, relationships, patterns. Mathematics
asks impossible questions and invents entirely new *thought* to solve them.

Why do so many people hate it? Because school mathematics commits the same
crime as teaching Shakespeare by assigning a quiz about grammar, or by teaching
art by color-by-numbers.

It throws you a corpse and calls it an animal. Like... sure... it is (or at
least *was*) an animal, but many of the things that make it an animal or
exciting in any way aren't there. Real mathematics is alive. It's playful,
dangerous, sometimes just straight-up rude.

A mathematician isn't someone who calculates. That's the job of a calculator,
and calculators have no personality. *Please stop asking mathematicians to
multiply large numbers, that's not what they do.*

There are two types of mathematics which mathematicians fall into either
bucket of.

There's the sort that introduces itself at dinner parties. "Hello, I'm calculus
and I help your rockets fly, teach computers things, and explain why your
coffee gets cold".

Then there's abstract nonsense. It is what happens when mathematicians stare
into a mirror for so long that the mirror starts taking notes.

## The Door Was Open, So Naturally We Went Through

We've spent the last few thousand years opening increasingly ominous doors.
First arithmetic, then algebra, then calculus, then set theory. Each one
stranger than the last. Each one step closer to a branch of mathematics so
abstract, so universal, so offensively elegant that it makes all other math
look like finger painting.

Mathematics is, arguably, the study of relationships. As anyone who has ever
dated will tell you, relationships are complicated and yet so so beautiful.

Most branches of mathematics are concerned about things that are reasonable to
conceptualize. "Is a triangle a triangle in all spaces?", "are there some
undeterminable numbers?", "how am I supposed to know what to substitute into
this integral to solve it, nobody taught me this, is everyone just winging
it?".

Then these great apes decided to just ignore the things entirely and focus on
how they relate. They made **category theory**.

Category theory is the language of madness.

Not necessarily the *bad* kind of madness. Not *"I can hear voices in the walls
telling me I'm pretty"* madness. Moreso the sort of madness that occurs when
the human brain – having invented shapes, numbers, equations, sandwiches –
decides what it really needs is mathematics about mathematics about
mathematics.

Category theory instead observes the master's hands work as several hundred
puppets dance on a fiberboard stage. It's the study of actions, so much so that
category theory doesn't even look at the puppets. From observing those
arithmetical fingers it deduces; "ah, Judy just paddled Mr Punch".

One can argue that civilization is built on the idea that things matter as much
as the actions of those things. This isn't true in category theory. Alice isn't
as important as the fact Alice owes Bob $20, *which one can suppose is also
true with banking*.

## At First, It Was Just Arrows

How mathematicians actually *define* categories begins with something mildly
suspicious. They start with "objects", which is odd because the whole sales
pitch was that we actually care about relationships between things... not the
things themselves. Yet here the things are. Objects. Sitting around, existing,
like they own the place.

Then come the "actions". These are one-way connections between objects. You can
think of them as arrows, or functions, or throwing a ball, or sending a message
you'll regret in twelve minutes.

Formally, for any two objects, there is a collection of these arrows going from
one to the other.

Informally, it's just things... doing things... to other things.

Which feels reassuring, at least until you realize something unnerving. You
don't actually *need* the objects. You can, with enough effort and a concerning
amount of free time, define everything purely in terms of arrows. Just
relationships, all the way down. No "things" required, just structure floating
quietly in the void, pretending it has something to attach to.

But this makes everyone uncomfortable, so mathematicians do what any sane
human would do and put the objects back in. They clear their throat and write
"a collection of objects" for the *n*th time that year. Because it's nicer that
way. Cleaner. Less existential.

## Truth (Unique Up To Unique Isomorphism)

A gnome in your garden of logic decided to crossbreed the truth flowers with
the falsity flowers and skip away giggling at the utter clusterfuck he left
behind.

> *"Tee-hee, some things are neither false nor true!"*

The law of excluded middle is, in this case, an anti-gnome missile... but it
leaves you with a boring garden like many of your neighbors. We call these
boring gardens *Boolean*, because their truth and falsity statements act like
the normal Boolean algebra that insufferable computer scientists don't shut up
about.

If, however, you happen to have a fun garden, then yes the gnome is correct in
that truth and falsity are not exclusive. You can have something be *not true*
but that doesn't necessarily make it false.

The single constructivist reading this is likely feeling validated; except for
the fact that non-constructive terms were used in the writing of the previous
sentence. If you wanted to prove that something is false because it's not true,
you'd first have to prove that true and false are the only existing states and
that they're exclusive.

## A Brief, Unfortunate Whistle-Stop Tour of Categorification

Right now is about the point where someone from the back of the room speaks
through the awkward coughs and silence with "so what can we actually do with
this?".

A 'fun' go-to is the relatively easy (but relativistically *quite* difficult)
area of quantum mechanics. At some point a mathematician looked at these
particular processes on probabilistic particles and decided "yeah that's just
a symmetric monoidal category lol lmao" as easily as brushing off a lobster
roll as just a sandwich.

I'm quite hungry.

Sounding like a minor villain from a low-budget space opera, ZX-calculus is a
categorical language for quantum mechanics that... *and I cannot emphasize the
'just' here enough*... just works.

These silly little collections of arrows and symbols ends up predicting
observed behavior. Compositions match experiments, and all produced by a
category theorist with a stick of Hagoromo and no fear.
